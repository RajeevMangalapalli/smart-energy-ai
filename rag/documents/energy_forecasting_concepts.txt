ENERGY DEMAND FORECASTING: CORE CONCEPTS

Introduction:
Energy demand forecasting is the predictive science of estimating future electricity consumption. It is a critical component of power system management, ensuring that supply meets demand in real-time while maintaining grid stability.

Types of Forecasting:
- Short-Term: Hours to days (Operational planning).
- Medium-Term: Weeks to months (Maintenance scheduling).
- Long-Term: Years (Infrastructure and policy planning).

Primary Drivers of Energy Demand:
1. Seasonality: Energy usage exhibits strong periodic patterns. Daily patterns reflect human routines, weekly patterns show lower demand on weekends, and annual patterns highlight heating/cooling needs.
2. Weather: Temperature is the most significant exogenous variable. Extreme cold increases heating load, while extreme heat increases air conditioning load.
3. Economic Activity: Industrial production and commercial operating hours significantly impact the base load.

The Importance of Accuracy:
Overestimating demand leads to wasted energy and higher costs, while underestimating can cause grid instability or blackouts. Modern forecasting leverages historical patterns (autocorrelation) and external features to minimize error.
